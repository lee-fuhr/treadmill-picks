How to choose the right treadmill

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best treadmill for you depends on your budget, available space, and running style. Focus on continuous-duty motor horsepower (CHP) over peak power, a deck length of at least 55 inches for walking and 60 inches for running, and a sturdy frame. Avoid under-powered ‘budget’ models if you plan to run regularly; mid-range machines from Sole, Horizon, or NordicTrack often hit the sweet spot for durability and features without breaking the bank.

What type of treadmill is right for you?

The first big fork in the road is foldable vs. non-foldable. Foldable treadmills save floor space and are great if you plan to tuck the machine away after every workout. The trade-off is that folding mechanisms add complexity and sometimes a slight compromise in frame stiffness, though quality brands like Sole and Horizon have engineered very stable folding decks. For serious runners who never fold, a non-folding treadmill usually feels more solid and often allows a longer warranty on the frame. You also have manual vs. motorised. Manual treadmills are cheaper, quieter, and don’t require electricity, but they force you to work harder to keep the belt moving and aren’t ideal for interval training. Motorised treadmills dominate the market for a reason: they give you precise speed control, incline, and a consistent running surface. Unless you’re on a strict budget and don’t need programmability, go motorised.

What motor power do you actually need?

Motor power is one of the most misunderstood specs. Look for continuous-duty horsepower (CHP), not peak horsepower. A CHP of 2.5 to 3.0 is sufficient for most walkers and joggers. If you plan to run more than 20 miles a week or weigh over 200 pounds, a CHP of 3.0 to 4.0 is worth the investment. Motors rated below 2.0 CHP are prone to overheating and premature wear if used for running. Reputable brands like Sole, NordicTrack, and Bowflex list CHP clearly. Avoid generic ‘2.5 HP’ claims that don’t specify continuous duty. A motor that struggles to maintain speed will ruin your workout and shorten the machine’s life.

How much space do you need for a treadmill?

Even a compact treadmill needs about a 6-foot by 3-foot footprint when in use, plus clearance on both sides and behind you. For running, a deck of at least 55 inches long is needed for walking, and 60 inches is strongly recommended for running. Shorter decks feel cramped and can cause you to step on the rear roller, which is a safety risk. Don’t forget ceiling height. If you’re tall or plan to run at an incline, your head needs clearance. A good rule of thumb is to allow at least 6 inches above your full stride height. Foldable models can cut the footprint in half when stored, but measure the folded dimensions and check if the machine is easy to roll away.

What features matter most for comfort and safety?

Cushioning is the big one. A treadmill with good shock absorption reduces stress on your knees, hips, and lower back. Brands like Sole and NordicTrack use flexible deck systems or elastomer cushions. A machine that feels too hard or too bouncy on a test walk should be crossed off the list. Incline matters even if you don’t plan to sprint uphill. A motorised incline of at least 10 percent gives you hills for variety and calorie burning. Decline options are rarer and useful for simulating downhill terrain, but not essential. For safety, look for an emergency stop clip (key) and a wide, non-slip belt. Heart-rate monitoring via handlebar grips is fine for casual use, but chest straps or arm-band sensors are far more accurate.

Should you buy a smart treadmill or a basic one?

Smart treadmills come with built-in screens, subscription workout libraries, and connectivity to Peloton, iFit, or Zwift. They keep you entertained and can auto-adjust speed and incline based on the class. The downside is cost – you pay a premium for the screen – and ongoing subscription fees that can add up over years. Many smart machines lose their ‘smartness’ within a few years if the software stops being supported. A basic treadmill with a simple LED display and a tablet holder is often a smarter buy. You can stream classes on your own device without recurring fees. If you love the convenience of a full screen, consider machines that let you cast your own content. The choice comes down to whether you want all-in-one convenience or long-term flexibility.

How to shop for a treadmill on a budget?

You do not need to spend several thousand dollars to get a reliable machine. In the ‘budget-friendly’ category (roughly the lower end of the market), brands like Horizon, Schwinn, and Sunny Health & Fitness offer decent walkers with solid basic features. Be realistic: very cheap treadmills often have weak motors, short decks, and less cushioning. They work fine for walking but will struggle with regular running. If you can stretch your budget a little into the ‘mid-range’ tier (a few hundred more than budget) you gain significant durability, longer warranties, and smoother operation. Look for sales on previous-year models from Sole or NordicTrack. Refurbished units from reputable retailers can also save money, but make sure the warranty is intact. Your best value is often a no-frills machine with a strong motor and a long deck, even if it lacks a big screen.

Frequently asked questions

How long should a treadmill last?

A well-maintained treadmill from a reputable brand typically lasts 7 to 10 years for light to moderate use, and 5 to 7 years for heavy daily running. The motor is usually the first major component to fail; investing in a 3.0 CHP or higher motor from a brand like Sole or NordicTrack helps extend that lifespan.

Can I use a treadmill on carpet?

Yes, but only on low-pile, firm carpet. A thin rubber mat underneath the treadmill is essential to prevent overheating and damage to the carpet fibers. High-pile or plush carpet is a fire risk because the motor needs airflow. If in doubt, place the treadmill on a hard floor or a dedicated treadmill mat.

Do I need a subscription to use a treadmill?

No. Many treadmills work perfectly without any subscription – you can use manual mode, free apps, or your own entertainment. Subscription-based features, like auto-adjusting iFit or Peloton classes, add value for some users but are optional. Always check if the machine is usable without a paid plan before buying.

What is a good weight capacity for a treadmill?

A weight capacity of 300 pounds or more is a good baseline for durability and safety. Machines with a higher capacity tend to have stronger frames, wider belts, and more powerful motors. If you weigh near or above that number, aim for a model rated at least 350 pounds from a rugged brand like Sole or Bowflex.

How often should I lubricate the belt?

Most manufacturers recommend lubricating the deck every 3 to 6 months for moderate use, or more often if you run daily. Check your owner’s manual for the specific silicone lubricant they recommend. A squeaky or sticky belt is a sign it’s time to lubricate, and ignoring it can wear out the deck and motor prematurely.

Is a treadmill workout as good as outdoor running?

Yes, in terms of cardiovascular benefit and calorie burn, treadmill running is very similar to outdoor running, provided you set a slight incline (1 to 2 percent) to compensate for the lack of air resistance. Treadmills are easier on the joints thanks to the cushioned surface, making them a safe choice for recovery days or injury prevention.

In shortBuying a treadmill is an investment in your health, so take the time to match the machine to your body, your goals, and your home. Prioritise a strong motor, a deck that fits your stride, and a warranty that covers at least a few years. A mid-range model from a trusted brand will almost always serve you better than a bargain-priced unknown. If you can test a few machines in person, do it – the feel of the belt and the cushioning is something no spec sheet can capture.
