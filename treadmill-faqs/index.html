Treadmill questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Treadmills vary widely in motor power, deck size, cushioning, and features, but most buyers should focus on a continuous-duty motor of at least 2.0 CHP, a belt that fits their stride length, and reliable incline. This guide answers the most frequent questions so you can shop with confidence.

What size motor do I really need?

Motor power is measured in continuous horsepower (CHP), and it’s the single most important indicator of a treadmill’s longevity and performance. For walking or light jogging, a 2.0 CHP motor is sufficient for most casual users. If you plan to run regularly, look for at least 3.0 CHP. Beware of peak horsepower ratings, they’re misleading. A 2.5 CHP motor that runs smoothly at low speeds is far better than a 3.5 CHP motor that struggles under load. The best way to judge is reading owner reviews after several months of use, not the spec sheet alone.

How important is incline and decline?

Incline is a vital feature because it simulates outdoor hills and boosts calorie burn without increasing impact. Most treadmills offer up to 10 to 15 percent incline, and many budget-friendly models include it. Decline (negative incline) is less common and found on higher-end machines. If you’re training for outdoor running, even a moderate incline of 3–6 percent makes a noticeable difference. For casual walkers, basic incline is helpful but not essential. The real question is whether you’ll use it, if yes, pay a little more for a model with quick-touch incline buttons rather than manual adjustment.

Which cushioning system is best for joints?

Cushioning reduces the impact on your knees, hips, and lower back, but too much cushion can feel unstable for running. The best systems absorb shock without making the deck feel bouncy. Many reputable brands use elastomer technology or variable-zones that are softer at the front and firmer at the rear. There’s no single “best” cushion, it’s subjective. Listen to what experienced runners say in reviews: if a treadmill is described as “too soft” or “like running on sand,” it might slow you down. If it’s called “too hard,” you’ll feel every step. A good rule is to test the treadmill in person if possible, or read multiple long-term owner accounts.

Should I get a folding or non-folding treadmill?

Folding treadmills save floor space, a huge advantage for apartments or multi-use rooms. The trade-off is that the folding mechanism adds complexity and sometimes reduces long-term stability. Some well-regarded folding models are impressively sturdy, but all folding treadmills have a hinge that can wear over time. Non-folding treadmills are typically heavier, more stable, and last longer because they have fewer moving parts. If you have a dedicated workout room, a non-folding deck is usually a better investment. For anyone who needs to roll the treadmill out of the way after each use, a folding model with a strong hydraulic lift is worth the compromise.

How often do treadmills need maintenance?

Regular maintenance is straightforward and extends the life of the treadmill by years. The deck and belt need lubrication every three to six months, depending on usage. Many newer models come with pre-lubed belts, but you’ll eventually need to apply silicone-based lubricant underneath. You should also tighten the belt if it slips, vacuum under and around the motor to prevent dust buildup, and check the power cord for damage. Most problems arise from lack of lubrication, not mechanical failure. A good habit is to lubricate at the same time you change your air filter or do another seasonal task.

What should I expect to pay for a quality treadmill?

Quality treadmills generally start in the mid-range price tier and go up significantly from there. Very inexpensive machines tend to have weak motors, short belts, and limited warranty support. For a durable walking treadmill, you’ll likely need to spend a few hundred dollars. For a running-capable model with a strong motor and good cushioning, the price climbs higher. Used treadmills can be a good deal if you inspect them carefully, look for rust, belt wear, and strange motor noises. The best value often comes from tried-and-tested brands with solid customer service, regardless of the price tag. Avoid no-name brands and read the fine print on the warranty before buying.

Frequently asked questions

How fast do treadmills go and do I need that speed?

Most home treadmills top out at 8–12 mph. That’s plenty for jogging and most runners. Unless you’re an elite sprinter, 10 mph is more than you’ll ever use.

What is the maximum user weight a treadmill can support?

Weight limits are usually listed between 250 and 350 pounds. Heavier users should choose a model with a higher rating, as it indicates stronger frame and motor components. Always check the spec for advertised weight capacity.

Do I need a subscription for the workout programs?

Many newer treadmills offer free built-in workouts, but premium services like iFit or Peloton require a monthly subscription. Decide ahead how much you want to pay ongoing. Some users prefer a simple manual treadmill and use their own tablet for entertainment.

How noisy are treadmills?

Noise depends on motor quality and deck construction. Well-made treadmills are noticeably quieter than cheap ones. A 3.0 CHP motor with a good belt is much less annoying than a budget motor. If you share walls, consider a model with noise-reducing cushioning and a quiet drive system.

How long do treadmills typically last?

With proper maintenance, a mid-range treadmill can last 5–10 years in a home with regular use. Budget models often wear out in 2–3 years. Higher-end commercial-grade units can last 15 years or more.

Can I put a treadmill on carpet?

Yes, but you need a treadmill mat to protect both the machine and the carpet. The mat prevents dust and fibers from being sucked into the motor and provides a stable, level surface. Without one, the treadmill may rock or overheat.

In shortWhether you’re a walker, jogger, or runner, the right treadmill comes down to matching the motor power, deck size, and cushioning to your real habits, not the flashiest touchscreen. Focus on long-term reliability, read credible owner reviews, and ignore exaggerated peak-horsepower claims. A well-chosen treadmill will serve you for years, not just the first few months.
